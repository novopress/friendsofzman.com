Herrman #34268 May 26, 2017 9:06 am 4
One of the areas of biological evolution open to serious debate is the pace of change. The standard model is a slow (very slow) continuous process with very subtle differences between individual “links” in the evolutionary chain. The idea that evolution could, or did, happen in relatively quick spurts, without a lot of intermediary steps, is probably best captured by Gould’s punctuated equilibrium. In response to rapid environmental change, species will have a corresponding rapid phylogenetic change. It’s a tough thing to tease out of the fossil and geologic record.If true, or at least a significant part of the picture, it would seem to make sense that subpopulations of critters could rapidly evolve in response to self-inflicted environmental stresses, like moving from a cold climate to a warm one or visa-versa. Given enough subgroup change, and fast enough, distinct but closely related species could evolve in multiple places on the planet at the same time in time spans measured in centuries instead of millennia. Might make for some interesting social dynamics between the groups once they come back together.
