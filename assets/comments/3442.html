As a German, let me thank you for bringing these developments to the attention of your readers.Some added details that might interest you: The movement started in the city of Dresden, the second city after Berlin in what used to be the GDR, and that still is where the biggest crowds assemble (the 17,500 you quote). However, there are also now franchises so to speak in a number of other (West) German cities, though they are much smaller in scale.The fact that these protests always take place on Mondays is symbolic as well. During the final years of the GDR, when the socialist structures were already crumbling and when Gorbatchev had signaled that the USSR this time likely wouldn’t intervene violently, so-called Monday demonstrations were being held regularly in many cities of the GDR, demanding more civic rights, the freedom to travel etc. (calling overtly for the overthrow of the regime or of socialism would have been to risky). So it’s into this framework and continuity of civic protest against a ruling elite that the PEGIDA movement is placing itself. This also accounts for the regularity of the event, on each Monday afresh, emulating the GDR protests of 25 years ago.We’ll see how it all plays out. Nothing definitive is won yet, but some cracks are appearing in the Cathedral, and they may be widening.
