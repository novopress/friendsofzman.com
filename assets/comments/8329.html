I came across that Caesar quote a while ago and it didn’t sound right to me, so I tried to track it down. It’s all over the internet without any specific citation. I had no great luck then, but I’ve figured out what’s up.This evening I first found that it’s earliest attestation (so far as I could discover) was in a book from 2003 by William. B. Whitman entitledThe Quotable Politician. Then I tracked down the origin from which this garbled quote comes.It’s from a passage in Cicero’sDe OfficiisBook Three, in which he argues that it’s impermissible for anyone to act unjustly in the pursuit of power. He likens such people to Caesar, who was even willingly (so says Cicero) to take on as his father-in-law the person through whose outrageous behavior he would acquire power. This of course alludes to the deal whereby Caesar married his daughter Julia to Pompey as part of their arrangement in which Pompey helped Caesar gain the consulship of 59 BC so that Caesar could then help Pompey get his way in legislation (though retrospectively Cicero interprets their actions more broadly as a take over of the state).As for the quote, it actually is attributed toPompey, and it’s in fact Cicero’s own translation of Euripides,Phoenissae, ll. 524-5. In the play, Oedipus has cursed his two sons and to get around this, they cut a deal to alternate in the kingship of Thebes. Eteocles gets to be king first, but when his year is up, he refuses to give way to his brother. In the Greek, what he says translates as, “If it is necessary to act unjustly, it’s best to act unjustly for power. In other regards, it’s necessary to act piously.”Cicero’s translation (by his own admission a bit clumsy, but written for clarity) goes:Nam si violandum est jus, regnandi gratia / violandum est jus. Aliis rebus pietatem colas(3.82). “For if right must be violated,it’s for the sake of rule that it must be violated. In other matters you should cultivate piety.” That’s pretty close to the Greek.As for the English quote attributed to Caesar, it’s reasonably close to Cicero, though it does have a rather different conclusion. In any event, as already stated, Cicero attributes that thought not to Caesar but to Pompey. And while Cicero says the lines were always “in his mouth”, no doubt he’s simply attributing to Pompey the idea expressed by Eteocles in Euripides’ play. I doubt very much that Pompey was constantly muttering these unsavory lines of Greek to himself (and the translation is overtly Cicero’s).Whether the misattribution and modified sense are to be ascribed to that Whitman guy or precede him, I have no idea. But this is an example of the frequent phenomenon on the internet where a quote that’s either entirely made up or garbled is attributed to somebody famous and gets repeated again and again without a proper citation.Anyway, Caesar never said anything of the kind.
