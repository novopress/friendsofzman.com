The jury remains out on the role of economic trends on the French Revolution. Tocqueville makes the point that the long term trend was for improvement, others, citing unemployment brought about by the Eden-Vergennes treaty make opposing points. The main trigger was the state of finances (France was broke).If you take Pierre Goubert seriously when he says that the real rift lay between a composite elite made up of upper bourgeoisie and old nobility against everyone else, you have something similar to what we see here now. The French elite didn’t really have a problem with the way things were, they just wanted to be in charge. Any appeals to lower classes were mere power plays. Thus you had the situations where parlaments were demonstrating against decrees of the king, them being sent into exile, and returning to popular acclaim when they won, or a compromise was reached. Declaring that the king nor his assemblies of notables had any right to change how taxes were structure or collected, and saying that this was the role of an Estates General, had a popular appeal in that the Third Estate would have a say, but their intentions were revealed when they insisted on the Estates voting by order, not by head, in the manner of the last one held in 1614. In doing so they revealed their contempt.If you throw out the bad harvests and severe winters that accompanied all of this, the coinciding food riots and Great Fear would not have happened, and the appeals to the urban lower classes to fetch the king back to Paris would have fallen on deaf ears. And what’s more, even if they had successfully gained power, there would have been no night of August 4 when they denounced feudalism (while keeping the most important rights of theirs intact). Again, they had no problem with the system as it was, they just wanted to be the ones running it.Which brings us to today. We have a system hurtling toward bureaucratic and managerial absolutism. The only flaws we have are the fact that people can still vote.
