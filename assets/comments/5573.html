I think that the “stock market” analogy of citizenship is a little strained. Suppose you own shares of a business. If the company sells additional shares *at the market price* you should probably be indifferent to the capital raise. If the company has $100 of assets and 100 shares outstanding, the per-share value of the company is $1. If they sell 100 new shares to investors at $1 per share, the assets and share count both increase, such that the per-share value remains the same.Now, what I think you’re getting at is the problem if a secondary offering at a reduced price. If the company in the example above we to sell the 100 new shares at $0.75 each, the per-share value of the company is now $0.875 — meaning that the folks who were the pre-existing shareholders are worse off, and the new buyers are instant winners.Perhaps a better way to frame your argument is that the current citizens of a country are the current shareholders, and immigrants (illegal or otherwise) are the folks buying in the secondary offering at a discounted price. They get the benefits of ownership without somehow paying a fair price.
