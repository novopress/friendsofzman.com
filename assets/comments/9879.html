I work for a 100 yr old manufacturer, we deal in custom engineered products for the petrochemical and other related markets, steam turbines and gas compressors. Technology has changed us and our product over the years, but slowly. We were, in the 1930s a very horizontal company. We had our own foundry, a tooling company, and a wide berth of markets and products – as did our competitors around us. There were about 5 or more of us in the US alone before 1970, now ther are two and we are both foreign owned.Over time specialization of manufacturing and markets trimmed us to a more vertical and focused company. Today we outsource foundry and some machining processes. Our products last 50 to 75 years back then and today may give a bit longer life service.So we are an old brick and mortar company that is a not interesting for many young techies. However that will change. High tech didn’t pass us by, but didn’t offer us great advancements. Advancements came in three places:1) computer processing. For drawings to Finite Element Analysis. Engineers can do more than before due to processing power. Franckly they do more. Computers allow to look at engineering more detailed than what was economic prior, so our products are better engineered, more sound.2) Manufacuring. Processes: From machining to welding there were continual small advancements. Today tolerances are tighter and welding methods better, meaning higher efficiencies, less vibration and more reliability.3) Materials: We went from Pig Iron, to Carbon steels to stainless and alloys. Better metal means better suited for service, more Reliablity.All these advancements came slowly.I think now that high tech has gone from infancy to adolescences we will see more quick advances in our industry. Printing components are not cost efficient today, but they will be in about 10 yrs, newer materials and other Manufacuring methods will also likely advance and maybe even take us away from metals.What I want to leave you with is that high tech has made minimal advances to older tech. I think both industries will now likely come into closer harmony and will reinvigorate the US rust belt in the coming years..

In the late ’60s I was introduced to a pyramid scheme where I worked. Simply put, you bought a ‘share’ which allowed you to sell four shares to people. You kept half the ‘sale’ and passed half on up to the person ‘above’ you, the one who had sold you a share. They in turn would be passing a share up the ladder one place.It was of course productless scam: there was nothing but an agreement to follow the rules. I made, and this is incredibly modest by today’s standards, some £28 but the people I sold four shares to as per the rules were destined to make less than a tenner each. Below that they were merely paying up the ladder and not finding any ‘buyers.’ I knew one of those guys and he even tried to persuade me to buy one of his shares but I just laughed and said no thanks. Remember, this was very local but by the time the potential market ran out (and the ‘scheme’ collapsed) it already required as many people as lived in the district to participate. I wasn’t math-literate but even I could see it was soon going to exhaust the number of participants sooner or later.At that point I understood an important business model we were to see replicated in the internet years: get in early, don’t worry about making anything other than promises, make people think they are buying something valuable to them and then, with their cash in your pocket, get out early while there is still a naive belief in promises washing around.The point was this was all short-lived, and the trick was to get in early enough to keep the money. It was all probably declared illegal not long after because no one floated anything like that at work again.
