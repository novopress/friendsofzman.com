UKer #89562 February 4, 2019 4:54 pm 4
When I worked in newspapers, almost twenty years ago, we knew for a while the sales were shrinking simply because people were dying. Much of the loyalty to the product was simply old people who had been brought up with having newspaper in their home (even though as Mr Z points out, a lot of the attraction was gossip and sports). Younger people who never read the paper had no interest.Even the ‘hatch, match and dispatch’ column of classified ads were destined to run out because people were getting married or having kids who didn’t feel the need to tell everyone with a paid-for advertisement when Fecesbook and the like would do it for free to their ‘friends.’ Once old readers began dying off there were naturally fewer people needing to read if old Fred from down the road had expired.There was also the television factor, so every newspaper for a time offered excellent coverage of programs on the TV, which was odd because TV was going to be a factor in crushing newspapers. Once TVs began to have easy to access program guides, the final ace card the papers held was gone.We had another issue with an evening newspaper I worked on. People heading home after work might stop driving to buy a newspaper. Once extensive parking restrictions (in the UK, the discovery of gallons of yellow paint that could be applied to roads to allow people to be fined for non-crimes) people couldn’t stop driving so a brief window of sales opportunity disappeared.There were other factors — notably the collapse of communism in eastern Europe which allowed more newspapers in previously restricted areas of the world, so the price of newsprint (the paper) was driven up, making it all more expensive to produce– but the writing was on the wall a long time before I left the business.
