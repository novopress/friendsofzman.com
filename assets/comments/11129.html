I ascribe most of the “fake news” to the urgency to be the first to break a story.In the halcyon days of newspapers (and I’m simplifying here to make a point), there were at least two major newspapers in any given major city. Stories, columns, opinion pieces, etc., had to be finalized by around 10 PM so that the typesetters could do their magic in order to produce a physical paper before dawn, to meet morning deliveries. If a reporter got wind of a story during the day, he generally had some time to make a few phone calls, double-check some facts, and maybe bounce the gist of the story off a few folks whose opinion he trusted. If the story were big, he’d also have to bounce it off the senior editor for a sanity check, and maybe also get an in-house legal opinion. Then the story would get typed up and finalized before the evening deadline. The reporter then crossed his fingers and hoped the rival paper hadn’t also picked up on the scoop. Because, if your paper got the scoop, people would buy copies of the paper in order to read about it. So there was also a money angle.In today’s internet age, there’s no time to double-check things. The urgency to be the first one to “get the scoop” is can be measured in seconds, since information is everywhere, and legions of other reporters are scavenging for scoops. Thus, the pressure to be the first one to announce a news story leads to running with half-assed information. There’s that military saying “the first report from the front is often wrong.” That’s what’s happening in today’s news — there’s no time for reflection. People are running with what they hear, which unfortunately is usually hearsay or rumor. The “72-hour rule” to allow the facts to present themselves has no place in the contemporary news cycle.And thus we get “fake news.”
